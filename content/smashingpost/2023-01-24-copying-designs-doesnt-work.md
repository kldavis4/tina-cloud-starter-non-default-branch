There‚Äôs a type of cognitive dissonance that haunts designers: the genuine desire to be truly innovative and original whilst simultaneously trawling the web for inspiration and design ideas.

I doubt many designers sit down with the intention of copying Airbnb, Apple, or Stripe. But they‚Äôre such beautifully-designed experiences that it‚Äôs hard *not* to be inspired to some degree &mdash; even subconsciously.

Whilst the limitless optionality of a blank Figma board can be overwhelming, the answer is rarely to copy someone else. The plagiarism issue aside, it‚Äôs **tough to replicate the magic of another user experience and not lose the magic that makes it work**.

*Do you actually understand why it‚Äôs worked? Have you lost the secret sauce somewhere in the replication? Can you then innovate your own ideas onto them?*

Firstly, let me give you an example of an industry where it‚Äôs been commonplace to copy what works from younger and more agile start-ups: ***banking***.

Today I published some of the findings from a 900-day study of 12 banks in the UK called The [UX of Banking](https://builtformars.com/ux-of-banking). As a lifelong reader of Smashing Mag, I wanted to give you all a modified excerpt from [that article](https://builtformars.com/case-studies/ux-banking-chapter-8) and then some advice on *how to effectively take inspiration from others*.

## Monzo Bank Was Heavily Copied In 2020

Back in 2020 (when I started this study), I was critical of how most of the incumbent banks handled basic card management features. They lacked context, the designs themselves were really poor, and there were basic usability issues.

New features, like freezing your card or showing your PIN inside the app, were simply added as new rows &mdash; similar to a settings page. This was the predictable risk of adding new functionality for decades without rebalancing the core user experience.

As an analyst (and at first glance), it might look like Metro has the best user journey. But in this instance, the number of clicks is the wrong metric to care about.

There appears to be very little positive correlation between what I believe are the better experiences (e.g., Revolut, Monzo, and Starling) and the number of clicks.

Rather, having too few clicks is more likely to be an **indication that there‚Äôs not enough context** (or that it‚Äôs crammed into one screen). 

**It‚Äôs almost like the obvious cheat codes of design: use more padding, space things out, and give yourself more room to add context.**

Let‚Äôs consider a few nuances to this specific user journey (i.e., losing your bank card):

- üìÖ **Infrequent usage**  
Most people aren‚Äôt ordering new cards every month. So, this isn‚Äôt a ‚Äòpower feature‚Äô, and they‚Äôre not hoping to optimize for efficiency.
- üò∞ **Negative connotations**  
Most of the time, you‚Äôre ordering a new card due to a negative event (e.g., stolen, lost, or damaged). You‚Äôre probably already anxious, stressed, and frustrated.
- ‚åõÔ∏è **Time sensitive**  
People need a new card quickly. The context behind delivery speed is important, rather than the speed of *ordering* the card.

**To summarize**: People who‚Äôve lost their card will likely be looking for reassurance and comfort rather than efficiency. That‚Äôs the tone of the user journey, and as a designer, you need to be acutely aware of it.

Metro has so few clicks because it‚Äôs essentially one screen with two buttons. You have no option to keep the same PIN (say, if you‚Äôve damaged your card at home), which means that you have to wait for both a card and a PIN to arrive.

The two clicks saved by not asking about this preference forced me to wait an additional two days before I could use my card.

- How quickly can they use their card (quantitative)?

If you were to copy Revolut‚Äôs flow but remove the features your product doesn‚Äôt have, that 11-click process might not feel the same.

Or, you could take inspiration from Monzo‚Äôs ‚Äòcard-selection screen‚Äô, but if you‚Äôve combined it with another step they don‚Äôt have, you may lose the magic of why it works.

Even banks, who largely have the same feature set, are replicating the same ideas with a really broad quality of execution.

## The Do‚Äôs And Don‚Äôts Of Implementing Inspiration

To be absolutely clear, I‚Äôm an advocate for leaning into the **Familiarity Bias** &mdash; a tendency for people to prefer (i.e., choose) products, services, and options that feel familiar to them. 

So, here are a few handy *do‚Äôs* and *don‚Äôts* of building *familiar-feeling* products, without losing that magic spark:

**‚úÖ Things to do:**

- **Try to identify the [psychological biases](https://builtformars.com/ux-glossary) that are in play.**  
Is the user frustrated? Sad? Are they trying to avoid making losses, or is their decision-making skewed by something else? Ultimately, do they resemble the same biases affecting your product?
- **Can you lean into a well-known mechanism and save some onboarding effort?**  
For example, TikTok popularized the ‚Äòswipe to watch‚Äô mechanism. There‚Äôs no shame in using that same navigation style; your users will instantly feel more familiar with your product. Remember: familiarity is a shortcut to learning.
- **Be inspired by the inventiveness of the execution, not the visual stuff**.  
It‚Äôs not productive to fall in love with Shopify‚Äôs checkout process and convince yourself that you need to copy it. Instead, try to identify how they‚Äôve cleverly innovated about subtle and specific problems (i.e., managing global delivery addresses in one page without reloading).

**‚ùå Things to avoid:**

- **Don‚Äôt outright copy someone else‚Äôs design.**  
You are probably only seeing 5% of the complexity, and the other 95% may include unexpected nuances that led to visible decisions.
- **Don‚Äôt assume that you should have feature parity.**  
In other words, just because a company you admire has features X, Y, and Z in the main nav doesn‚Äôt mean you should. *What do your users actually want to do?*
- **Don‚Äôt forget that your start-up isn‚Äôt Airbnb, and you need to educate people on what you actually do.**  
In other words, when a product becomes a household name, they often don‚Äôt need to spend time (i.e., clicks) teaching the user what they actually do &mdash; everyone already knows. But you probably do.

I don‚Äôt have the silver bullet of successfully replicating the magic of Apple (and if I did, honestly, I‚Äôd probably keep it to myself and make a fortune). 

But, hopefully, this serves as an important reminder not to follow the competition blindly.
